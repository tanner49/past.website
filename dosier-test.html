    Candidate Statement

    Introduction

    ​

    My goal is to become an interdisciplinary researcher across the research areas of learning analytics and instructional design. As a interdisciplinary scholar I will perform impactful research that advances the development and use of learning analytics instructional tools, teach in areas related to my research, and serve others in my field through mentorship, service to professional organizations, and service to my institution. The primary goal of my research is to equitably share the power of learning analytics with all learners through the creation of more generalizable (and thus more scalable) learning analytics-based instructional tools. I am working to development a unique scholarly perspective in this area of research by fostering a broad, interdisciplinary understanding of methods, theories, and applications across instructional design, learning analytics, artificial intelligence in education, and other related research areas. As an instructor, I aim to help students foster the ability to critically evaluate a broad range of information related to their individual professional contexts. I believe that modern success is often determined by the ability to make connections across a variety of diverse areas of knowledge (Siemens, 2005), and to determine a personal, adaptive course of learning based on these connections (Blaschke, 2012). It is these skills that I attempt to foster in students.

    ​

    Goals for Scholarship

     

    Early access to learning analytics instructional studies and tools is restricted to those with the affluence necessary to collect large amounts of data, which is often dependent on access to internet-connected devices, preferably at a 1:1 ratio (Yu, 2020). It has been suggested that this ability to access data may be leading to a second digital divide (United Nations, 2019). Much research suggests the solution to this problem is to procure and more equitably distribute funding (Bailey et al., 2012; Karar, 2019; Keane & Keane, 2020; Sundeen & Sundeen, 2013; Tsai et al., 2019). However, others suggest that the advancement of learning analytics tools is dependent on the design of cheaper, more intuitive, and more scalable learning analytics tools (Buckingham Shum et al., 2019; Shum & Mckay, 2018; Wise, 2014).

    ​

    Despite this recognition by learning analytics researchers, little primary research has been done to achieve the goal of better instructional tools. In my scholarly work, I focus on the intersection of three related research gaps that may account for this lack of instructionally-focused learning analytics research:

    ​

    1. Learning analytics lacks interdisciplinary researchers. Learning analytics is a multidisciplinary field (i.e. scholars from different disciplines working together) that pulls from data science, artificial intelligence, statistics, learning science, and many other fields. Because there a few researchers who began their careers as learning analytics researchers, learning analytics tend to have focused expertise in either learning analytics methods or learning analytics theory (Dawson et al., 2014). In other words, there are few interdisciplinary researchers. This same phenomenon was observed in the multidisciplinary field of cognitive science, and the lack of integrated researchers within the field led to the development of opposing factions, and eventually the decay of multidisciplinary efforts (Núñez et al., 2019). To advance learning analytics research, there is a need for interdisciplinary researchers who have a more general knowledge set across both methods and theory, allowing for the synthesis of a cohesive vision for the advancement of learning analytics.

    2. Learning analytics needs instructional design researchers. Although the first decade of learning analytics research has focused on the development of methods and theory, there is increased interest within the field on how to design pedagogically sound learning analytics instructional tools and increase adoption of these tools (Phillips & Ozogul, 2020; Wise, 2014). However, because most learning analytics researchers have backgrounds in learning science or computer science, there are not enough researchers to adequately advance this line of research. Currently, research on the advancement and adoption of learning analytics tools predominantly focuses on building relationships with practitioners (Tsai et al., 2019), not on the actual design process of creating learning analytics tools.

    3. Learning analytics privileges expensive learning context. Currently, learning analytics research tends to be performed in fully online settings where data collection is easy. Because of this, most research is performed in either online higher education courses or MOOCS (Kovanović et al., 2018; Phillips & Ozogul, 2020; Wise & Cui, 2018), where data collection is easy. While this has allowed for the rapid development of methods and theory, it has also led to a field with a limited scope. Many methods require a 1:1 student-to-device ration, meaning under-served learning context cannot benefit from learning analytics research. I have a personal interest in extending learning analytics research to under-served learning contexts to increase educational equity. 

     

    My primary research focus area. These three gaps together have led to a roadblock in creating more equitable and scalable learning analytics tools. Because researchers are not interdisciplinary, they often pursue research agendas built around their specific methodological strengths, not around solving specific problems. There are especially few researchers with experience or interest in instructional design. Because of this lack of interest in instructional design, research tends to be completed in settings most convenient to researchers, not in authentic learning contexts. This has led to the exclusion of under-served learning settings from learning analytics research.

    ​

    My personal research agenda treats the inequity in learning analytics as a design problem. Because learning analytics is a mainly quantitative field, I see methodology as the main design tool that researchers must gain fluency in to advance the field. By gaining a broad understanding of statistical, machine learning, and artificial intelligence methodology and grounding this understanding in instructional theory, I hope to offer a uniquely interdisciplinary vision of the future potential of learning analytics instructional tools to generalize to diverse contexts.

    ​

    First Authored Study. My first authored study, titled Artificial intelligence as a tool for teacher reflection on curricular material in diverse contexts, utilizes state-of-the art methods in deep learning to investigate how curricular material and graded student material can be used to aid in teacher reflection on instruction. Data was collected from an online introductory biology class at a large private U.S. university during the fall of 2019. Automatically generated transcripts of recorded lectures were used to teach an AI instruction tool the linguistic space of the class material. The AI was then exposed to the text of assignments, quizzes, and exams, as well as student’s performance on this material. Using this information, the AI instruction tool was able to detect lecture material related to the assessment items that students performed poorly on. The below figure displays a graph of the estimated difficulty of lecture material over the course of the entire semester. This information can be used at many levels of detail to determine specific units, lectures, or even paragraphs of text that contain information students struggle with. This information could be used to develop a dashboard for teacher reflection on instructional material.

    The impact of this study lies in its advancement of (a) equitability in learning analytics (b) methods for natural language processing of small data sets and (c) linkage between artificial nbsp;intelligence methods and schema learning theory (Derry & Deny, 2011; Rumelhart, 1984). This study advances equity in learning analytics by presenting a method for analyzing instructional material that does not require access to large amounts of data or a large quantity of devices. To implement this instructional tool, instructors need only a single internet-connected device and a microphone. No student technology is required. This study proves the efficacy of several natural language processing methodologies including skip-gram-generated word embeddings and random-deletion data augmentation. The study also acts as an example and a catalyst for disucssions around the connection and reconciliation of schema theory in the cognitive tradition (Rumelhart, 1984) and the constructivist tradition (Derry & Deny, 2011).

    Plans for Professional Development.

    To continue my scholarly growth, I plan on continued growth in my understanding of learning analytics theory, instructional theory, and quantitative methodology.

    ​

    Competency 1: Learning analytics theory. I continue to build my understanding of learning analytics theory through study, publication, and participation in scholarly communities. I have attended the Society for Learning Analytics Research summer institute, published research related to learning analytics, and am part of a research team submitting a piece to the LAK` 2021 conference. Because of learning analytics relatedness to learning science, I have included learning science coursework in my program of study and perform research in a multidisciplinary group of learning science, instructional technology, and computer science researchers.

    ​

    Competency 2: Instructional theory. I continue to pursue an academic understanding of instructional design principles and theories. Before entering the PhD program, I had a practical understanding of many instructional design principles through my work as an instructional designer, and I excelled in my completion of the core master’s coursework in IST. I have also published research [CITE] that explores the relationship between learning analytics and instructional design research. I have been appointed as a junior editor of the International Journal of Designs for Learning, under Professor Elizabeth Boling and continue to learn about the design process through that service.

    I have also worked closely with other design scholars. I am co-author with Dr. Ahmed Lachleb on a proposed chapter for A Practitioners Guide to Instructional Design in Higher Education entitled Instructionally-focused Learning Analytics as a Tool for Reflection in Instructional Design Practices (As of of September 1st, 2020, the proposal for this chapter has been approved, and we are currently in the process of writing the full chapter). This process has allowed me the opportunity to explore further the relationship between learning analytics and instructional design. My work as a research assistant has also allowed me to observe and begin to participate in the design process of a computer-support collaborative learning environment. In the future, I will continue to develop my competencies in instructional design through (a) my appointment as a research assistant and (b) the extension of my early research to applied instructional applications.

    ​

    Competency 3: Quantitative Methodology. By pursuing a minor in statistics, I am continuing to develop a strong understanding of standard statistical methods, as well as the mathematical theories behind quantitative analysis. This understanding of mathematical theory has been invaluable as I extend my learning to other quantitative methodologies. By working closely with computer science researchers at North Carolina State University, I have begun to develop a better understanding of machine learning and artificial intelligence methodologies, especially in the context of natural language processing. I plan to continue my learning by completing a masters in statistics in addition to my minor, and by continuing to work closely quantitative researchers where possible.
